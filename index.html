02 Market-Moving Headlines
Apple's Best-Ever March Quarter Drives Nasdaq to Record Close
›
Equities Macro

AAPL capped the week with a blowout fiscal Q2 beat: revenue of $111.2 billion (+17% YoY) and EPS of $2.01 (+22% YoY), both above consensus, powering shares up ~3–4% on Friday and lifting the NASDAQ to a new record close of 25,114.44. iPhone sales surged 22% to $57 billion — a March quarter record — while Services hit an all-time high of $30.98 billion. CEO Tim Cook described iPhone 17 demand as "extraordinary" and said supply constraints, not demand, limited the quarter.

Apple guided June quarter revenue growth of 14–17%, far above analyst expectations of ~9.5%, and the board authorised an additional $100 billion in buybacks alongside a 4% dividend increase. The lone shadow: Cook warned of "significantly higher memory costs" in Q3 and beyond, with gross margin guidance of 47.5–48.5% — below Q2's 49.3%. Cook will step down as CEO on September 1, handing the reins to hardware chief John Ternus.

The result reinforced a strong earnings season. XOM and CVX both beat estimates, META halted its selloff, and ORCL jumped ~7% on continued AI-driven demand. The SPX sealed a 10.4% April gain — its best monthly performance since November 2020 — and is now at all-time high valuations.

Knock-on Effects
Memory Supply Chain: Cook's explicit warning on rising memory costs spotlights SSNLF (Samsung) and MU (Micron) as key risk vectors; AI-driven HBM demand is crowding out consumer DRAM.
Services Narrative Intact: With Services at $31bn and growing 16%+, the high-margin flywheel reinforces Apple's case for premium valuation multiples despite iPhone supply headwinds.
Ternus Transition Risk: Markets will begin pricing the CEO handover premium; expect analyst notes reassessing Apple's AI strategy with Google Gemini powering Siri under new leadership.
Nasdaq Concentration: The index is now at record valuations driven by a narrow set of Mag-7 names — Berkshire's Buffett warned of a "gambling mood" at Saturday's annual meeting.
Source: CNBC, Apple Newsroom, MacRumors
Trump Breaks Turnberry Deal, Hits EU Autos with 25% Tariff — Effective This Week
›
Macro Equities FX

President Trump announced Friday via Truth Social that he will raise tariffs on EU cars and trucks to 25% this week, citing the EU's failure to comply with the July 2025 Turnberry Agreement. The move comes under Section 232 national security authority and overrides the existing 15% ceiling negotiated last year. The EU Commission flatly rejected claims of non-compliance and warned it will "keep options open" to protect EU interests.

The context makes the announcement particularly destabilising: the Turnberry deal was already undermined earlier this year when the Supreme Court ruled Trump lacked emergency powers to impose the original tariff levels, effectively reducing the ceiling to 10%. Germany — responsible for a large share of EU auto exports via BMW, MBG (Mercedes-Benz), and VOW3 (Volkswagen) — faces the sharpest blow. Shares of F fell ~2% and STLA dropped ~1.7% in the wake of the announcement; GM fell 1.5%.

The escalation lands at a precarious moment: the global economy is already grappling with Iran war-driven oil inflation (+75% WTI YTD), with the IMF having cut its 2026 growth forecast to 3.1% and raised global inflation expectations to 4.4%. EU trade experts warned this is "the first enforcement action" against a major trade partner and urged Brussels to respond "with the utmost clarity and firmness."

Knock-on Effects
EUR/USD Pressure: Euro rebounded to 1.1723 on Friday but faces renewed headwinds if retaliation threats escalate into a broader trade war; watch for EU countermeasure announcements this week.
European Automakers: BMW, MBG, and VOW3 all import significant US-destined volumes from European plants — margin compression and production-shift decisions will dominate their next earnings calls.
US Inflation Layering: Combined with oil above $100/bbl, auto tariffs add another cost-push inflation layer; the Fed's on-hold posture through 2027 looks increasingly justified.
USMCA Overhang: Automakers have privately told the White House they won't shift production until the USMCA formal review in July is resolved — meaning short-term disruption without reshoring gain.
Source: Bloomberg, CNBC, Euronews
Iran War Powers Deadline Passes — White House Claims Ceasefire 'Terminated' Hostilities
›
Commodities Macro

Friday, May 1 marked the 60-day War Powers Resolution deadline since Trump notified Congress of military action in Iran on March 2. Rather than seeking congressional authorisation or withdrawing forces, the White House declared that the ongoing ceasefire has effectively "terminated" hostilities — a novel legal argument that Defense Secretary Hegseth summarised as "the 60-day clock pauses or stops in a ceasefire." Congress went on recess Thursday after the Senate rejected, for the sixth time, a Democratic-led war powers resolution (failed 50–47), with Maine Republican Sen. Susan Collins breaking ranks for the first time.

Oil markets were volatile on the news: WTI fell nearly 3% to $101.94/bbl on Friday after Iran reportedly submitted a revised peace proposal via Pakistani mediators, though Trump said he was not satisfied with the terms. Brent fell ~5% to $108.17/bbl. Despite Friday's pullback, both benchmarks are up ~75% YTD and ~60% since the war began Feb. 28, driven by the near-total closure of the Strait of Hormuz. The IEA has called the disruption the "greatest global energy security challenge in history," with over 10 million bpd of Gulf output offline at peak.

Sen. Lisa Murkowski (R-AK) said she will introduce a formal AUMF when the Senate returns the week of May 11 if the administration has not presented a plan. Prediction markets (Kalshi) assign >50% odds that WTI will hit $127/bbl at some point in 2026, and a 63% chance of $120/bbl.

Knock-on Effects
Oil Upside Risk Persists: The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed and the US Navy blockade continues; any ceasefire breakdown would immediately send Brent back toward $120+.
Inflation Pass-Through: Dallas Fed modelling suggests the current Strait closure path could add 1.7 ppts to annualised headline CPI — cementing the Fed's on-hold posture through 2026 at minimum.
Constitutional Showdown Delayed: With Congress in recess until May 11, the legal standoff is deferred but not resolved; a Murkowski AUMF would force the Senate's hand in mid-May.
Energy Equity Bifurcation: US producers (XOM, CVX) benefit from record crude exports; airlines and logistics names face a structural cost shock with Spirit Airlines having already ceased operations citing fuel costs.
Source: CNN, CBS News, 1440
Berkshire's Post-Buffett Era Begins: Abel Signals Patience on Record $397bn Cash Pile
›
Finance Equities

BRK.B held its first annual meeting without Warren Buffett on stage on Saturday in Omaha, with new CEO Greg Abel presiding over what has been dubbed the "Woodstock for Capitalists." Berkshire reported Q1 operating profit of $11.34 billion (+18% YoY) and net income of $10.1 billion (more than double year-earlier levels). The company's cash pile swelled to a record $397.4 billion, up from $373 billion at year-end 2025. Attendance was notably lower this year — the arena was just over half full.

Abel struck a Buffett-like tone on capital deployment: "We're not anxious to deploy capital into subpar opportunities," he said, signalling continued patience despite the record war chest that represents more than 50% of Berkshire's investment portfolio. He said Berkshire would approach AI "in a narrow scope focused on creating value" and acknowledged AI-driven deepfake cybersecurity risks as a key challenge for its insurance business. Geico reported a 34% drop in earnings despite an overall insurance underwriting profit of $1.7 billion.

Buffett, seated in the front row as Executive Chairman, endorsed the succession and warned the audience of a market in a "gambling mood," noting valuations were unfavourable for deploying Berkshire's cash. The stock has trailed the S&P 500 by more than 30 percentage points since Buffett announced his departure last May.

Pentagon Signs Sweeping AI Deals with Seven Tech Giants — Anthropic Shut Out
›
Equities Macro

The US Department of Defense announced Friday that it has reached classified AI deployment agreements with seven technology companies: SPCE (SpaceX), MSFT, GOOGL, NVDA, AMZN (AWS), ORCL, and newcomer Reflection AI. The deals allow these firms to deploy LLMs across the Pentagon's most sensitive Impact Level 6 and 7 classified networks "for lawful operational use," with the stated goal of making the US military an "AI-first fighting force."

The announcement notably excludes Anthropic, which the Pentagon had previously labelled a "supply-chain risk" after the AI lab refused to remove safety guardrails against autonomous weapons and mass surveillance uses. Anthropic won an injunction against that branding in March but remains sidelined from the classified network programme. The process has been dramatically accelerated — integration that previously took 18 months has been slashed to under three months.

More than 1.3 million DoD personnel have already used the GenAI.mil platform, generating tens of millions of prompts. The 2026 DoD budget includes $33.7 billion earmarked for science, technology, and autonomous systems. AI capex across the hyperscalers is on track to approach $700 billion in 2026, per Charles Schwab — with the Pentagon now the largest single institutional AI consumer.

Knock-on Effects
NVDA / MSFT / GOOGL Upside: Classified government AI contracts provide high-margin, long-duration revenue with strategic moat — expect upward revisions to federal segment revenue for all seven firms at next earnings.
Anthropic Valuation Risk: Being locked out of the largest institutional AI buyer while simultaneously in legal dispute with the Pentagon creates a significant overhang on Anthropic's reported $61.5bn valuation and any IPO timeline.
AI Ethics Precedent: The Pentagon's willingness to bypass guardrails on autonomous weapons use sets a global precedent; expect European regulators and AI safety advocates to intensify scrutiny this week.
UiPath / Enterprise AI Angle: As the Rundown AI notes via UiPath CMO Michael Atalla, 70–80% of agentic AI initiatives fail to exit pilots — the Pentagon's war-footing integration timeline tests that thesis at scale.

UiPath CMO: 70–80% of AI Pilots Never Exit — Orchestration Is the Fix
›

In an exclusive interview featured in The Rundown AI, PATH CMO Michael Atalla — five years after UiPath's IPO — argues that the core problem plaguing enterprise AI is not ambition but coordination failure. "ROI disappears in the gap" between disconnected AI tools and broader business goals, he said. The company has repositioned from robotic process automation to "agentic business orchestration" — connecting AI agents, automation pipelines, and human workflows end-to-end.

Atalla drew a parallel to the cloud transition he led at Microsoft with Office 365 in 2011: companies that "lifted and shifted" without redesigning workflows failed, and the same pattern is repeating with AI today. He noted that approximately half of organisations now call AI a "massive disappointment" despite heavy investment — a coordination problem, not a technology problem. The lesson for enterprise buyers: ask not "what can this model do?" but "what does our workflow need to look like for this to work?"

Why It Matters
With AI capex heading toward $700 billion in 2026 and the Pentagon accelerating classified AI deployments, the enterprise adoption gap Atalla describes has direct market implications: companies that crack orchestration stand to compound returns across every AI tool they deploy, while pure-play model vendors face commoditisation pressure.
Source: The Rundown AI

Why It Matters
With the Strait of Hormuz still effectively closed and negotiations described by Trump as far from resolution, the structural oil bid is intact. Any peace deal breakdown — or resumption of US strikes — would likely send Brent back toward its $126 wartime high, threatening another CPI shock heading into summer and increasing pressure on the Fed to remain on hold well into 2027.

Why It Matters

Oscars Set AI Rules for 2027; Only Human Performances and Scripts Eligible
›

The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences released new eligibility rules for the 2027 Academy Awards, requiring humans to be at the center of creative processes. Only performances by humans will be considered for acting awards, and only human-authored scripts may receive screenplay awards. The rules also expand international film eligibility as Academy membership becomes more diverse globally.

The decision reflects mounting industry anxiety over AI-generated content threatening the livelihood of performers, writers, and directors — anxieties that underpinned last year's SAG-AFTRA and WGA strikes. The ruling creates a practical enforcement challenge: defining where AI assistance ends and human authorship begins. Separately, the Academy's timing coincides with a wave of AI film tools reaching commercial maturity, making the boundary increasingly contested.

Why It Matters
The Oscars ruling sets a cultural and legal marker that other IP-intensive industries (publishing, music, gaming) are likely to follow. For AI content companies, it signals regulatory risk at the premium end of creative markets — though enforcement gaps may allow AI-assisted production to thrive in mid-tier and streaming content where awards eligibility is less critical.
