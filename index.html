02 Market-Moving Headlines
Apple Posts Best-Ever March Quarter; $100B Buyback Sweetens the Deal
›
Equities

AAPL delivered a blowout fiscal Q2, reporting revenue of $111.2 billion — up 17% year-over-year and a record for the March period, with diluted EPS of $2.01 (+22% YoY), topping Wall Street's $1.95 estimate. iPhone revenue surged 22% to nearly $57 billion, a March-quarter record, though it narrowly missed the $57.21 billion consensus as supply constraints — not demand — bit into shipments. Services hit an all-time high of $30.98 billion (+16% YoY).

The quarter's headline surprise was guidance: Apple told analysts June-quarter revenue would grow 14%–17% year-over-year, more than doubling the 9.5% consensus estimate. The company authorized a fresh $100 billion buyback and raised its quarterly dividend 4% to $0.27/share. CEO Tim Cook called iPhone 17 "the most popular lineup in our history," while flagging that memory costs — driven by AI chip demand — will become an "increasing impact" on margins through the rest of 2026.

The earnings call also marked the first appearance of incoming CEO John Ternus, who will succeed Cook on September 1. Shares climbed more than 3% on the week, helping lift the Nasdaq to a new record. The conglomerate's largest holder, BRK.B, separately reported $11.34B in Q1 operating earnings with a record cash pile, as new CEO Greg Abel debuted at the Omaha annual meeting.

Knock-on Effects
Memory-cost contagion: Apple's warning that AI-driven memory shortages will weigh on margins in coming quarters amplifies similar flags from Meta and Microsoft, pointing to a sector-wide margin headwind.
Buyback tailwind: A new $100B repurchase authorization adds a structural bid under AAPL, which remains Berkshire's largest equity position.
Leadership transition watch: John Ternus's September ascent shifts investor focus toward Apple's AI strategy — R&D is already up 33% — and whether the new CEO will accelerate or pivot the company's deliberate approach to generative AI.
Supplier read-through: Strong iPhone 17 demand, supply-constrained at TSMC, is a positive read-through for TSM advanced-node capacity; negative for any rival competing for the same wafer allocation.
Source: CNBC, Apple Newsroom, MacRumors
Trump Declares Iran Hostilities ‘Terminated’ as War Powers Clock Expires — Oil Slides
›
Macro Commodities Politics

Friday marked the 60th day of Operation Epic Fury, the constitutional deadline under the 1973 War Powers Resolution for President Trump to seek congressional authorization or begin withdrawing forces from Iran. Instead, Trump sent letters to House Speaker Mike Johnson and Senate Pro Tem Chuck Grassley declaring that the April 7 ceasefire had "terminated" hostilities — arguing the clock had stopped, not merely paused. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth previewed the argument in Senate testimony, though North Carolina's Sen. Thom Tillis and other Republicans pushed back.

Democrats rejected the legal theory outright. Sen. Adam Schiff noted that the U.S. Navy continues to enforce a blockade of Iranian ports while Iran simultaneously restricts Strait of Hormuz transits — a "dual blockade" that opponents say constitutes active hostilities. A sixth Senate bid to curb war powers was defeated 50-47, largely along party lines. Congress then entered a week-long recess, with Sen. Lisa Murkowski pledging to introduce an AUMF when lawmakers return the week of May 11 if the administration does not act.

Iran sent a fresh peace proposal through Pakistani mediators on Friday; Trump swiftly rejected it. On Sunday (May 3), Trump publicly stated the U.S. may consider resuming strikes on Iran, sending fresh tremors through energy markets. WTI crude fell nearly 3% on Friday to $101.94/bbl on ceasefire hopes but remains elevated as the Hormuz standoff persists.

Knock-on Effects
Oil volatility: Any resumption of U.S. strikes on Iran — or an Iranian closure of the Strait of Hormuz — could spike Brent crude well above $115/bbl; airline and consumer-goods stocks face the sharpest margin exposure.
Congressional AUMF risk: Murkowski’s threat to force a war-powers vote week of May 11 adds a political wildcard that could fracture Senate Republicans and generate headline risk for risk assets.
Safe-haven flows: Escalation rhetoric from Trump on Sunday could lift gold — already near $4,644/oz — and drive a flight into Treasuries, compressing the 10-year yield from 4.38%.
Defense sector: Uncertainty around strike resumption keeps LMT, RTX, and NOC in focus as geopolitical premium in defense names remains elevated.
Source: CNN, NBC News, Washington Post, CBS News
S&P 500 & Nasdaq Seal Best Month Since 2020 as AI Capex Debate Dominates Earnings Season
›
Equities Macro

April ended with a flourish: the S&P 500 gained 10.4% for its best month since November 2020, while the Nasdaq surged 15.3%, its strongest month since April 2020. The Dow added 7.1%. For the week ending May 1, the S&P and Nasdaq posted gains of 0.9% and 1.1%, respectively. With more than half of S&P 500 companies having reported, over 80% have beaten expectations, per CNBC, and the blended Q1 earnings growth rate stands at roughly 32% — the highest since Q4 2021.

The week’s dominant theme was the bifurcation in how markets are treating AI capital expenditure. GOOGL jumped 10% after raising its 2026 capex guidance to as much as $190 billion alongside a strong revenue beat. By contrast, META and MSFT fell 8.6% and 3.9% after their own capex hikes drew scrutiny over near-term returns. ORCL jumped nearly 7% as AI infrastructure spending signals found a more receptive audience.

Analysts at Schwab note that AI capex industry-wide could approach $700 billion in 2026, and "markets are rewarding AI spending that shows near-term monetization" while punishing outlays without clear incremental returns. The VIX ticked up modestly but remains below 17, a nearly two-week low, suggesting the market is optimistic but not complacent ahead of a packed week of jobs data and earnings.

Knock-on Effects
AI ROI scrutiny: Expect sharper demands for AI return-on-investment disclosure in Q2 reports; companies unable to show monetization timelines may face outsized sell-offs regardless of top-line growth.
Memory & semiconductor supply: The AI capex arms race is driving a global memory shortage that is simultaneously a tailwind for chip names (NVDA, MU) and a margin headwind for device makers like Apple.
Breadth concern: Despite index-level records, decliners are outpacing advancers 1.5-to-1 on a weekly basis; only 50% of S&P stocks trade above their 50- and 200-day moving averages — a warning sign for the rally’s durability.
Small-cap divergence: The Russell 2000 (+0.46% Friday, best April since 2020) is outperforming on bets that domestic-revenue companies are less exposed to Iran-driven energy cost spikes.
Source: CNBC, Schwab, FactSet, TheStreet
Berkshire’s Abel Era Begins: $397B Cash Hoard, AI Cybersecurity Warning, No Buffett at the Podium
›
Equities Finance

BRK.B CEO Greg Abel presided over his first annual shareholder meeting in Omaha on Saturday, marking the end of Warren Buffett’s six-decade reign at the podium. Berkshire reported $11.34 billion in Q1 operating earnings and disclosed a record $397 billion cash and Treasuries pile, which Abel framed as a strategic asset: "We do not intend to be beholden to anyone." Buffett, now chairman emeritus, watched from the audience and told CNBC that Abel is "doing everything I did and then some, and doing it better in all cases."

Abel stressed that Berkshire would use technology and AI to "add value" across its sprawling portfolio — including GEICO and BNSF — but would not "do AI for the sake of AI." In a striking moment, a deepfake video of Buffett opened the Q&A session, which Abel used to flag AI-driven cybersecurity as a significant risk the firm manages "every day." Berkshire has trailed the S&P 500 by more than 30 percentage points since Buffett signaled his retirement last May.

With the record cash hoard and Abel’s hints at a faster trigger on bolt-on acquisitions in energy and industrials, investor focus will now center on whether the new CEO accelerates capital deployment — or continues Buffett’s famously patient approach — particularly with equity markets at all-time highs.

Knock-on Effects
Capital deployment signal: The $397B cash fortress is both a market indicator (Buffett found equities too expensive) and a potential market-moving catalyst if Abel signals a major acquisition.
AAPL stake watch: Berkshire’s largest equity holding is Apple; Abel’s commentary on the position — hold forever or trim further — could move both BRK.B and AAPL on Monday.
Insurance & rail read-through: Abel’s focus on GEICO efficiency and BNSF technology upgrades is a read-through for the broader insurance sector and rail equities.
Governance precedent: How Abel manages the dual role of CIO and CEO — overseeing 90%+ of investments alongside operating companies — will set a governance template watched by conglomerate investors worldwide.
Source: CNBC, Reuters, CNN

Macro Bonds

The Federal Reserve held rates steady at 3.50%–3.75% at its April/May meeting — the last chaired by Jerome Powell — with CME FedWatch showing a near-zero probability of a June cut following Wednesday’s decision and Thursday’s data. More notably, odds of a 2026 rate hike rose from zero to 6% in the aftermath of the meeting, reflecting the Fed’s increasingly hawkish tilt as Iran-driven energy inflation keeps core PCE pressures sticky. The 10-year Treasury yield closed at 4.378%, tracking higher alongside equities — an unusual co-movement that bears watching.

The week ahead brings the most important macro data of the month. April nonfarm payrolls (Friday, 8:30 AM ET) are forecast to rise just 73,000 — a steep deceleration from March’s 178,000 — with the unemployment rate expected to hold at 4.3%. Average hourly earnings are seen accelerating to +0.3% MoM. A miss on payrolls could reignite rate-cut chatter; a beat alongside strong wages could push hike odds higher and pressure long-duration assets.

Also on the week’s calendar: JOLTS job openings (Tuesday), ADP Employment (Wednesday), preliminary Q1 productivity and unit labor costs (Thursday), ISM Services PMI (Tuesday), and preliminary Michigan Consumer Sentiment (Friday), which is expected to remain near record lows as Iran-conflict fuel prices weigh on household confidence.

Knock-on Effects
Rate path repricing: A weak payrolls print could push the first cut back to Q4 or 2027; a strong number alongside elevated CPI keeps hike odds alive and could send the 10-year toward 4.60%.
Dollar sensitivity: EUR/USD sits at 1.1723; a hawkish surprise in Friday’s jobs data could drive a sharp USD rally, pressuring emerging-market currencies and commodity prices.
Kevin Warsh confirmation: The Senate may vote on Warsh’s Fed chair nomination this week — his confirmation would be the most consequential Fed leadership change in years, with markets pricing a more hawkish successor to Powell.
Consumer sentiment floor: Record-low Michigan sentiment tied to fuel prices is already showing up in retail sales softness; watch discretionary names (MCD, DIS, both reporting this week) for corroborating signals.

UiPath CMO: Most AI Projects Hit a Wall — Orchestration Is the Answer
›

Five years after its IPO as the category leader in robotic process automation, PATH has pivoted its pitch to "agentic business orchestration" — connecting AI agents, automations, and humans into end-to-end workflows with full visibility. In an exclusive interview, CMO Michael Atalla said the question enterprises ask has shifted from "can we automate this?" to "how do we get all of these things working together?" He drew on his 15 years at Microsoft shepherding Office to the cloud, arguing that most AI pilots fail not on technology but on integration: "technology should take friction out of people’s work, not add new kinds of it." AI job anxiety, Atalla noted, is real but nuanced — people worry about their paychecks even as workflows transform around them.

Why It Matters
As hyperscalers pour hundreds of billions into AI infrastructure, enterprise orchestration platforms like UiPath sit at the critical last mile — converting raw AI capability into measurable business outcomes. Investors skeptical of AI ROI should watch whether agentic orchestration metrics (not just ARR) become the new disclosure standard.

Innovation
Social Media’s Big Tobacco Moment: Juries, Bans, and a Mental Health Reckoning
›

The legal and regulatory noose around social media platforms is tightening. A California jury ordered META and GOOGL (Alphabet) to pay $6 million in damages to a woman who suffered depression and anxiety using their platforms as a teen, ruling the apps were intentionally designed to hook young users. New Mexico separately won a case against Meta, which was fined $375 million for misleading users about risks to children on its platforms. Legal experts say these verdicts create precedents for a cascade of future litigation. Phones are now banned in schools in 27 U.S. states and major districts including New York City, structurally reducing teenage platform engagement. Research shows reducing social media use to 30 minutes/day substantially lowers depression, anxiety, and insomnia rates in young adults.

Why It Matters
Landmark jury verdicts and accelerating school phone bans represent a structural shift in the regulatory and liability landscape for social platforms. Advertisers targeting teens face shrinking inventory; litigation exposure for Meta and Alphabet could become a material balance-sheet risk if precedents lead to class-action waves.

KEY THEME
Jobs Friday Could Remake the Rate Narrative
›

The week of May 4 is the most consequential macro week of the month, and possibly the quarter. April nonfarm payrolls — forecast at just +73,000, less than half of March’s 178,000 — will arrive Friday morning and hit a market that has just priced out nearly all Fed easing for 2026 while simultaneously watching a 6% probability of a hike emerge. A miss of that magnitude would reignite a dovish reprice of the front end; a beat alongside sticky wages could push hike odds into double digits and test the S&P 500’s record levels. Layered on top is a historic earnings slate — PLTR and AMD for AI credibility, DIS and MCD for consumer health, UBER for the gig economy — and unresolved Iran geopolitics that could spike oil at any moment. Investors who got used to markets climbing a wall of worry in April should prepare for a bumpier ride.

06 One Thing to Watch
One Chart to Watch: 10-Year Yield vs. S&P 500 Co-Movement
The unusual positive correlation between Treasury yields and equity prices — both rising together through April — is a yellow flag that deserves scrutiny this week. Normally, higher yields compress equity multiples; that they have not done so suggests the rally is being driven by genuine earnings upgrades rather than multiple expansion alone. Friday’s payrolls number is the pivotal test: if yields spike on a hot jobs report and equities fall in tandem, it signals the correlation is reverting to its historical norm and the record-high S&P 500 multiple faces a real compression risk. Watch the 10-year yield at the 4.40%–4.50% range as the first line of defense.
