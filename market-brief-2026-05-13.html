April CPI Hits 3.8%, Highest Since 2023 — Real Wages Turn Negative for First Time in Three Years
›
Macro Bonds

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported Tuesday that the Consumer Price Index rose 0.6% month-on-month in April, lifting the year-over-year rate to 3.8% — the highest since May 2023 and one-tenth above the Dow Jones consensus of 3.7%. The primary culprit was energy: the index for energy surged 3.8% on the month, accounting for over 40% of the total increase, with gasoline prices up 28.4% annually as the US-Iran war continues to choke the Strait of Hormuz.

Core CPI (ex-food and energy) climbed 0.4% MoM and 2.8% YoY — the hottest monthly core print since January 2025 — buoyed by a one-time shelter catch-up from the October government-shutdown data blackout, higher airfares (+20.7% YoY), and streaming-service price hikes. Food prices rose 0.5% for the month, with beef +14.8% and tomatoes +15% for the second consecutive month.

The wage picture turned ominous: real average hourly earnings fell 0.3% annually, the first negative reading since April 2023, meaning the cost of living has officially outpaced paychecks. The 10-year Treasury yield jumped 5 bps to 4.463% on the release, while the SPX dipped 0.16% to 7,400.96 and the NDX fell 0.71% to 26,088.20 before trimming losses into the close.

Knock-on Effects
Fed Path: CME FedWatch now prices ~30% odds of a rate hike by year-end; virtually no cuts are priced for 2026, pushing the first cut expectation to mid-2027.
Consumers: With real wages negative for the first time in three years, discretionary spending faces a squeeze heading into summer — a risk for retail and consumer-staples names.
Bonds: The 10-year at 4.463% and rising increases the cost of capital across equity valuations; watch duration-sensitive sectors like real estate and utilities.
Politics: A CNN poll found 77% of Americans — including a majority of Republicans — say Trump policies have raised their cost of living, adding policy pressure ahead of midterm season.

Fed in a Box: Four Dissents, Incoming Chair Warsh, and No Good Options
›
Macro Bonds

The April CPI print lands at a uniquely fraught moment for the Federal Reserve. In late April, the Fed voted to hold rates steady for a third straight meeting, but the decision drew four dissents — the most since 1992 — with Governor Stephen Miran voting for a cut and three regional presidents objecting to language perceived as signalling the next move would be easing.

Adding complexity, incoming Chair Kevin Warsh has publicly advocated for lower rates — a position now directly at odds with 3.8% headline inflation and a core rate of 2.8% that threatens to breach 3%. The Treasury market has priced out all Fed cuts in 2026, and the prospect of a hike — however remote — is creeping into derivatives pricing.

Economists warn the inflation trajectory will not quickly reverse even if the Iran conflict de-escalates, given supply-chain lag effects. Mark Zandi of Moody's Analytics expects inflation to keep accelerating into summer before easing toward 3.3% by year-end — meaning the Fed is likely frozen for months, caught between a softening labor market and an energy-driven price shock.

Knock-on Effects
Rate Hike Risk: A 30% market-implied hike probability is unusual; any further upside CPI surprise could force the Fed's hand and roil risk assets.
Warsh Confirmation: Senate confirmation hearings for Warsh will now be scrutinized for his inflation-fighting credibility after his dovish stance pre-dates the energy shock.

Hims & Hers Craters 14% After Surprise $92M Q1 Loss and GLP-1 Strategy Reset

HIMS plunged more than 14% Tuesday — closing at $25.03 — after the telehealth company reported a Q1 net loss of $92.1 million, swinging from a $49.5 million profit a year earlier. Revenue of $608.1 million, up just 3.8% YoY, also fell $8 million short of estimates.

The miss reflects a painful strategic pivot: Hims is exiting its lucrative compounded GLP-1 weight-loss drug business — which faced regulatory scrutiny — and shifting toward branded treatments, including a newly inked deal with NVO (Novo Nordisk) to sell Wegovy on its platform. On the bright side, management raised full-year 2026 revenue guidance to $2.8–$3.0 billion (vs. prior $2.7–$2.9B) and guided Q2 revenue of $680–$700 million — 6% above consensus. CFO Yemi Okupe told analysts the company expects to return to profitability in 2027.
