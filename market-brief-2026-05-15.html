02 Market-Moving Headlines
Warsh Era Begins at the Fed: Powell Steps Down as Chair Today
›
Macro Bonds

Kevin Warsh officially assumes control of the Federal Reserve today as Jerome Powell's four-year term as chair expires. The Senate confirmed Warsh in a 54-45 vote on Wednesday — the most partisan confirmation of a Fed chair in U.S. history — with only Pennsylvania Democrat John Fetterman crossing the aisle.

Warsh inherits a central bank under acute pressure: inflation remains elevated at 3.8%, well above the 2% target, and the Iran war has added persistent energy-cost shocks. Markets are currently pricing near-zero probability of a rate cut this year, with FedWatch odds not exceeding 2.8% for any single meeting. Notably, Powell is not departing entirely — he retains his seat on the Board of Governors through 2028, setting up a potential ongoing dynamic between the outgoing and incoming leaders.

Warsh's first FOMC meeting as chair is scheduled for June 16-17. He has previously called for "regime change" at the central bank and has pushed for a faster wind-down of the Fed's $6 trillion-plus balance sheet. Markets will parse his first public communications closely for any signal on the rate path.

Knock-on Effects
Rates: Near-zero cut probability priced through year-end; a hawkish Warsh pivot could send 2-year yields higher and pressure rate-sensitive equities.
USD: A more hawkish or reform-focused Fed posture is broadly dollar-supportive; watch DXY reaction to Warsh's first speech.
Equities: High-multiple AI and tech names are most vulnerable to a higher-for-longer repricing; financials could benefit from a steeper yield curve.
Political Risk: Powell remaining on the FOMC as a governor means Trump's push for rate cuts faces an ongoing institutional check, sustaining White House-Fed tension.
Source: CNBC, Morning Brew, CNN, NPR
Trump-Xi Summit Ends With Warm Words, No Breakthroughs — Markets Retreat
›
Macro Equities

President Trump departed Beijing Friday after a two-day summit with Xi Jinping that produced effusive praise from both leaders but few hard deliverables. No major trade agreements were signed, no firm commitments on reopening the Strait of Hormuz were secured, and Taiwan remained a flashpoint: Xi warned that mishandling the island question could lead to "clashes and even conflicts."

The summit's most tangible outputs were China's interest in purchasing more U.S. oil to reduce reliance on Middle Eastern crude, a joint statement that Iran must never obtain nuclear weapons, and vague language on expanding market access. Trump said Xi invited him to reciprocate at the White House in September. The summit was the first presidential visit to Beijing in nearly a decade.

Friday's U.S. equity session reflects the post-summit reality: NVDA -2%, AMD -3%, INTC -4%, and the Nasdaq shedding roughly 1.5% from Thursday's record close. Analysts note that tech's recent "extremely unsustainable" move leaves the group vulnerable to exactly this kind of profit-taking when geopolitical catalysts disappoint.

Knock-on Effects
Semiconductors: H200 chip sales to China approved in principle but no deliveries yet — NVDA remains exposed to any re-escalation of export controls.
Energy: Strait of Hormuz remains blockaded; no firm Chinese commitment to broker a reopening keeps crude elevated near $99/bbl.
Taiwan Strait Risk: Xi's blunt Taiwan warning injected a note of caution into defense and Taiwan-proxy equity plays.
Dollar & Treasuries: Post-summit 'risk-off' buying has Treasury yields dipping ~2bps as investors reassess the growth outlook.
