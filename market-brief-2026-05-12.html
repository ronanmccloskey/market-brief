02 Market-Moving Headlines
April CPI Shocks at 3.8% as Energy, Shelter & Tariffs Pile On
›
Macro Bonds

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported Tuesday that the Consumer Price Index rose 0.6% in April (seasonally adjusted) and 3.8% year-over-year — above the 3.7% consensus and the highest annual reading since May 2023. Energy alone accounted for over 40% of the monthly gain, as Strait of Hormuz disruptions kept crude elevated. Shelter costs rebounded 0.6%, signaling inflation is not confined to energy.

Core CPI (ex-food and energy) accelerated to 0.4% month-over-month and 2.8% year-over-year, topping the 0.3%/2.7% forecasts. Tariff-sensitive categories stung: apparel +0.6%, household furnishings +0.7%, airline fares +2.8% (up 20.7% annually). Real average hourly wages slipped 0.5% on the month and fell 0.3% annually — the first time in three years that inflation has fully erased wage gains.

CME FedWatch odds for a Fed rate hike by year-end jumped to roughly 30% after the print, with futures markets now pricing zero cuts in 2026. The Fed voted to hold at its April meeting with four dissents — the most since 1992 — reflecting a deeply divided committee. SPY futures fell sharply on the news; the 10-year Treasury yield climbed further above 4.41%.

Knock-on Effects
Fed Policy: Rate-cut pricing for 2026 has effectively evaporated; hike odds at ~30% by year-end per CME Group data, putting the FOMC in its most hawkish posture since early 2023.
Consumer Squeeze: Real wages negative on a 12-month basis for the first time in three years — a direct drag on discretionary spending and consumer confidence.
Bond Market: 10-year Treasury yields pushing above 4.41%, tightening financial conditions and pressuring rate-sensitive growth equities.
Earnings Risk: Higher input costs from energy and tariffs could begin compressing margins in Q2 2026 guidance, particularly for industrials, airlines, and consumer discretionary names.
Source: BLS, CNBC, Kiplinger
Stocks Retreat as Chip Rally Reverses; Nasdaq Falls ~1.5%
›
Equities

U.S. equities pulled back sharply Tuesday, ending a six-week winning streak at fresh records. SPX fell ~0.65%, the DJI shed ~0.03%, and the COMP dropped ~1.52% as the hotter CPI print hit high-duration tech names hardest. The selloff interrupted a record-setting run fueled by strong Q1 earnings across mega-cap tech.

Chipmakers and AI-linked stocks led losses. INTC fell 4.3–4.7% after more than doubling in the prior month. MU dropped ~4% following Monday's record close. CRWV slumped ~8%. On the upside, ZBRA surged 13–15% after beating earnings and raising its full-year profit outlook, while AMTM gained 3.4% on a quarterly beat. UA plunged ~19% on a wider-than-expected quarterly loss.

Q1 2026 earnings season is winding down with 89% of S&P 500 companies having reported; 84% beat EPS estimates — well above the 5-year average of 78%. The blended year-over-year earnings growth rate stands at 27.7%, led by Information Technology (revenue growth of 29%) and Financials. Analyst 12-month bottom-up target for the SPX implies ~16% upside from current levels.

Knock-on Effects

Trump Heads to Beijing: Energy Exports, AI & Trade Truce on Xi Summit Agenda
›
Macro Commodities

President Trump departed Tuesday for Beijing ahead of a May 14–15 summit with President Xi Jinping — his first China visit in eight years. Trump invited 16 top executives including TSLA CEO Elon Musk and AAPL CEO Tim Cook to join the trip. Trade, AI governance, and the Iran war are expected to top the agenda.

A key item under consideration: a deal for Beijing to resume purchases of U.S. energy. Tariffs imposed during the trade war have halted nearly all Chinese imports of U.S. oil and LNG — worth $8.4 billion in 2024 — creating a potential bargaining chip. Beijing recently invoked its anti-sanctions law to defy U.S. blacklisting of Chinese refineries buying Iranian oil, adding a fresh irritant ahead of talks.

The Iranian war pushed rates back above 6%, while inflation concerns weighed on buyer confidence. Sales had fallen 2.9% in March.

The national median existing-home price hit $417,700 in April — an all-time record high for the month according to the National Association of Realtors. Inventory rose to 1.47 million unsold homes, the highest for April since 2019, yet still well below the ~2 million pre-pandemic norm. NAR's chief economist Lawrence Yun said a 30% increase in inventory would be needed to bring buyers and sellers to more even terms.

Agents cited a frozen job market, steep prices, and inflation uncertainty as key buyer deterrents. Citi remains constructive on LOW ahead of next week's earnings, arguing the home improvement industry has "bottomed" and should outperform in 2026 despite macro headwinds.

Single HIV Infusion Shows Remission Up to 92 Weeks in Landmark Trial
›

Researchers presented data Tuesday showing that a single infusion of engineered HIV-fighting immune cells — modeled on a leukemia treatment — suppressed the virus to undetectable levels in a small trial. Scientists extracted and engineered seven patients' immune cells to carry two molecules that both bind to HIV and kill infected cells. Patients stopped daily antiretroviral drugs on the day of reinjection.

Those who began antiretroviral therapy early in their diagnosis saw remission lasting up to 92 weeks after switching to the infusion. Patients who started treatment later responded less effectively. Researchers cautioned the therapy remains years from widespread availability, but called it the closest yet to a one-and-done HIV treatment.

Why It Matters
A durable single-shot HIV therapy would be transformative for the ~39 million people living with HIV globally and could reshape the multi-billion-dollar antiretroviral drug market dominated by GILD, ABBV, and GSK — though the multi-year commercialization timeline limits near-term investment impact.
